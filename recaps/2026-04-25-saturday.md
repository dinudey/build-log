### Ethics and Trust — AI UX Ethics Analyzer

**What it was trying to learn**
Whether Claude can analyze UX design artifacts (Figma exports, HTML prototypes, PDFs) and assess them against AI ethics parameters in a way that's actionable for UX designers specifically — not researchers or compliance teams. A secondary question shaped the design: can the AI meaningfully distinguish what a designer can fix directly, what requires cross-functional buy-in, and what is structurally outside design's control?

**What was actually built**
A 71KB interactive HTML wireframe file exploring 4 layout directions × 3 screen types for an Ethics & Trust Analyzer dashboard. The 4 directions — Report Document, Dashboard Grid, Canvas Annotate, Split Inspector — were generated simultaneously as a design space rather than through single-concept iteration. Each direction runs across 3 screens: Overview, Deep-dive, and Scope.

The mock data uses a realistic checkout flow Figma PDF (14 parsed screens) scored against 10 ethics pillars: Fairness, Accountability, Transparency, Explainability, Trust calibration, Privacy, Consent, Bias & inclusivity, Error handling, and Dark patterns. The visual design uses a paper/sketch aesthetic — Caveat (handwriting) for headings, IBM Plex Mono for data, warm paper tones — deliberately signaling "working document" rather than finished product.

**Non-obvious decisions or tradeoffs**

The scope tricolor model is the sharpest design decision: each finding is tagged as one of three scopes — *scope-you* (moss green, designer can address directly), *scope-collab* (ochre, needs engineering, legal, or product), or *scope-out* (clay, structural or policy — outside design's jurisdiction). Most ethics frameworks implicitly make designers feel responsible for everything. Explicitly labeling things as out of scope is a relief valve: it helps designers escalate appropriately and stops them getting stuck on problems they can't solve.

The Socratic "ask" per finding is the other notable decision. Instead of listing fixes as instructions, each finding includes a reflective question alongside the recommendation (e.g. "What signal would help a user decide whether to accept this?"). This reframes the tool from compliance checker to design coach — treating output as a prompt for thinking, not a task list to clear.

Generating 4 directions in one session rather than iterating on one is an AI-native design move: use the model to open a design space quickly, then judge across directions rather than committing early.

**What it unlocked**
- The concept is visually expressible — you can now show what this tool looks like across 4 different interaction models
- The scope tricolor model holds up as a standalone framework for design teams navigating AI ethics responsibilities
- Proved the Socratic finding format works — it's a reusable pattern for any AI-assisted UX review tool
